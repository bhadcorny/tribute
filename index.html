Dr. Norman Borlaug

A man who save a billion lives
`

Dr. Norman Borlaug, third from the left,trains biologists in mexico on how to increase wheat yields - part of his life-long war on hunger.

Here's a timeline of Dr.Borlaug's life:
  • 1914-Born in cresco,lowa.
  • 1933-Leave's his family farm to attend the University of Minnesota thanks to a depression era program known as "The Youth Administration"
  • 1935-Had to stop school and save up more money.Works in the civillian conservation corps,helping starving americans. "I saw how food changed them",he said "all of this left this scars on me".
  • 1937-Finishes university and takes a job in the US forestry service.
  • 1938-Marries wife of 69 years Margret Gibson. Gets laid off due to budget cuts. Inspired by Elvin Charles Stakman,he returns to school to study under Stakman, who teaches him about pest-resistent plants.
  • 1941-Tries to enroll in the military after the pearl harbor attack, but is rejected. Instead,the military asked his lab to work on waterproof glue DDT to control malaria,disinfectant,and other applied science.
  • 1942-Receives a Ph.d in Genetics and Plant Pathology.
  • 1944- Rejects a 100% ssalary increase at Dupont,leaves behind his pregnant wife,and flies to mexico to head a new plant pathology program. Over the next 16 years,his team breeds 6,000 different of disease resistent wheat-including differnt varieties for each major climate on earth
  • 1945-Discovers a way to grow wheat twice each season,doubling wheat yields.
  • 1953-Crosses a short sturdy dwarf breed of wheat with a high yielding American breed,creating a strain that respond well to fertillizer.It goes on to provide 95% of mexico's wheat
  • 1962-Visits Delhi and brings his high yielding strains of wheat to the indian subcontinent in time to help migrate mass starvation due to a rapidly expanding population
  • 1970-Recieves the nobel peace prize
  • 1983-Helps seven african countries dramaticaly increase their maize and sorghum yields
  • 1984-Becomes a distinguished professor at Texas A&M University.
  • 2005-States"we will have to double the world food supply by 2050". Argues that genetically modified crops are the only way we can meet our demands,as we run out of arable land. Says that GM crops are not inherently dangerous because "we've been genetically modifying plants and animals for a long time. Long before we call it science, people we selecting the best breed".
  • 2009-Dies at the age of 95.
